Math in Early Life to Now

Water polo

Water polo is a sport that I have previously played competitively for many years. The sport is a combination of swimming, ball handling, and teamwork. It is very similar to soccer but in water.

Water polo is a sport that requires a lot of practice, mental planning, team play, and, more importantly, math, from calculating angles to estimating distances.

When playing I have to make sure I understand and are able to estimate the speed and trajectory of the ball using it's vectors to ensure that it goes into the goal. This requires a good understanding of both physics and mathematics.

For example, when shooting the ball, I must calculate the angles and distances to the goal. This requires a good understanding of both trigonometry and geometry.

This is especially important due to the interactions between the ball and the water. In the 3D-rendered image example, a shot with a spin bounces off the water and changes its direction rapidly.

Animation

One of the more interesting applications of mathematics I use is in 3D animation.
Using trigonometric functions, such as sine waves or quadratic functions, to create smooth and precise animations is an excellent example of how I use math when doing 3D animation and rendering.

Furthermore, one of my favourite things to use math for is texturing.
Various noise functions (such as Perlin and Voronoi) are used with multiple mathematical functions (such as range and multiplication) to adjust and manipulate noise during the texturing process.
I can then use mathematical processes to map these textures to objects.

Additionally, I use math to accurately position and rotate objects in 3D space, done using matrices and vectors;
by applying transformations such as translation, rotation, and scaling, I can create 3D models and animations that are visually appealing and realistic.

The images below show a couple of textures of node setups and the function inside to create this outcome.

These are the node setups I use to create the textures for the animation.

Image of the orange beam's textures node setup.

Multiple processes are happening in the images above.

First I am taking the objects 3d faces coordinates and mapping them to a 2d space (UV map) so that the texture can be applied to it accurately.

Then I am using a fractal perlin noise function to create a base texture, and then using a variety of math functions to manipulate the noise to create the final texture.

The math functions I use for this are either done though a standard mathematical node that can run, an operation function, a comparision, a rounding function, or a trigonometric function. I also commonly use a color ramp as a function for mapping values, where 0 is black and 1 is white. Using a coloramp makes visualing mathematical much easier and allows for easier modification of functions. It also also useful for mapping the mathematical functions into visual elements for the texture.

I don't always use a perlin noise function, I might use a type of graident function, a voronoi function, or a banded wave fuction(such as sine, cosine, or tangent).

Additionally pre-made images can be used in conjunction to make materials, as seen in the globe texture.

These functions together are used to create a variety of textures and materials.

Collision Blocks and π

One of my favorite mathematical phenomenon, that combines both my love of physics with mathematics, is the collisions of two blocks calculating the digits of π.

See the interactive demo below to understand how the number of collisions between two blocks— differing in mass by a factor of 100— reveals the digits of π. The demo uses a simple physics engine to simulate the motion of the blocks and their interactions.

Demo by Prajwal DSouza

This naturally occuring phenomenon is a great example of how math and physics in nature create unusual and fascinating results.

At first I didn't understand how this was possible, it made no sense to me why something semingly unrelated to π, would have some connection, let alone represent digits of π. However after looking for the solution, I could understand the connection when displaying calculating their momentum and thought that it was quite facinating.

The blocks are set in motion, and as they collide, they transfer energy and momentum. The number of collisions between the two blocks is directly related to the digits of π. This is because the formula for energy $\frac{1}{2}mv^{2}$, or more the conservation of energy between the two block $\frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^{2} + \frac{1}{2}m_2v_2^{2}=constant$, forms a circle when graphed substituting in x and y.

Mathematical phenomena like this truly fascinates me. They capture my attention in a way few other subjects do. Discovering unique and intriguing patterns in math has become one of the key reasons I've grown to genuinely enjoy the subject.
